# Ideate

A four-phase process for producing ideas worth having, not just the first three that come to mind.
The enemy here is **premature convergence** — anchoring on the obvious, judging while generating, and
mistaking variations of one idea for a real range. The phases deliberately separate *diverging* from
*converging* and put an adversarial coverage check between them. Work them in order.

## Phase 1 — Frame (Socratic)

Before generating, understand what a *good* idea even means here. Ask sharp, batched questions:

- **The real problem or opportunity.** What's the actual job to be done? The stated brief is often a
  solution in disguise — find the need underneath it.
- **What would make an idea valuable** in this context — and what would make one useless? This is the
  filter for Phase 4; name it now.
- **Constraints — and which to suspend.** Note the hard limits, but identify which constraints are
  worth *deliberately ignoring* during divergence to avoid strangling ideas at birth.
- **What's already been tried or considered**, so you can push past it rather than re-surface it.

Confirm the framing in one line, then diverge.

## Phase 2 — Diverge (generate wide)

Generate a lot, and generate *varied*. Defer all judgment here — no idea gets criticized in this
phase. Force range by working through genuinely different lenses, e.g.:

- **The obvious ones** — get them out so you stop circling them.
- **Different angles** — solve it from the user's view, the cost view, the speed view, the opposite view.
- **Borrowed solutions** — how has an adjacent field, product, or era solved a structurally similar problem?
- **Inversion** — what would guarantee the *opposite* outcome? Invert it.
- **Constraint flips** — what if you had 10× the budget? Zero budget? One day? No existing system?
- **Combinations** — fuse two earlier ideas into a third.
- **The deliberately wild** — at least a couple that feel too bold. They're seeds, not commitments.

Aim for quantity and spread. Group loosely as you go so the landscape is visible, but don't prune yet.

## Phase 3 — Challenge (adversarial coverage check)

Attack the *set*, not to kill ideas but to expose what's missing and what's fake range:

- **Anchoring.** Are most of these variations of a single underlying idea wearing different hats?
- **Missing categories.** What *whole class* of solution isn't represented at all? Name it and generate
  into the gap.
- **Fake novelty.** Which "ideas" are just the status quo rephrased? Mark them.
- **The unexamined obvious-best.** The idea that feels like the clear winner — why might it be wrong,
  or why has nobody done it already? Pressure it.

Generate another round to fill the gaps this surfaces. Good ideation usually has a second wind right
when it feels tapped out.

## Phase 4 — Converge & deliver

Now judge. Against the value criteria from Phase 1:

- **Cluster** the full set into themes.
- **Surface the most promising few** — say *why* each is strong, and what it'd take to test it cheaply.
- **Keep the wildcards** worth not losing, even if not chosen now.
- **Name the tension**, if the strongest ideas trade off against each other.

Deliver the shortlist plus the full set, and offer to save it (e.g. `ideas-<slug>.md`). The goal of
convergence is a small number of ideas worth *doing something about*, not a ranked list of everything.
